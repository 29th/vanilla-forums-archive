Corporal Terry N. York

Cpl. York has participated in his first official scrimmage and is therefore eligible for his Combat Infantry Badge, 1st Award. In addition, for being a part of a two round scrimmage in which both rounds were a victory he will receive the World War II Victory Medal.

Cpl. York had done particularly well throughout our training and come scrim day in serving as secondary squad leadership when called for, and in competently leading the squad through adversity as it came. Though he didn't need to lead the squad during the scrim, he made himself well prepared to and competently managed and worked with those around him in each round. On defense, Cpl. York had helped to keep the squad swift in reacting to the attack from the south-western fields. Along with this, he provided excellent protection to our combat engineer and worked in fluid coordination by his side. In the later minutes of the defense, Cpl. York and others had helped establish a defensive perimeter around the objective school building. Here, Cpl. York had worked to protect our combat engineer who hit an approaching M-ATV in the pond just south. He positioned himself well enough to eliminate all infantry disembarking the burning vehicle. By the end of the defense round, Cpl. York had eliminated a total of five enemies and kept himself, our squad, and the objective safe. Having eliminated more infantry than any other on the defense is impressive, and especially having done so in a leadership position whilst showing his capabilities in working competently with the unit as a whole. Cpl. York had done well in keeping apart from the SL on attack so as to avoid being eliminated simultaneously, and had again proven himself to be solid in combat as he worked with the squad to push all the way into and through the town and into the objective. While his actions on the attack reflected well, Cpl. York distinguished himself above all on defense in aiding towards victory with five kills.

Private, First Class Logan B. Albers

PFC Albers has participated in his second official scrimmage. For being a part of a two round scrimmage in which both rounds were a victory he will receive the World War II Victory Medal.

PFC Albers had done well to eliminate two M-ATVs on our defense round, after they aggressively pushed through our western defense towards the school building. He'd listened well to the calls on the vehicles and positioned well so as to be protected by the rest of the squad so as to stay alive. He reacted quickly to callouts on the vehicles to the south-west and reacted quickly while keeping safe among our infantry. Albers then proceeded to eliminate one M-ATV east the drainage ditch south of "pipe", placing a tandem to it's engine while others eliminated those who disembarked. He'd taken the order to fall back to the objective building and wait for vehicles to push in, and later nailed a second M-ATV in the engine as it moved on the pond near the objective. Albers eliminated two vehicles in the defense round, and knew where to be, and when to fall back and keep alive. I couldn't have asked more than that.

    Private, First Class Alex S. Turner

    PFC A. Turner has participated in his first official scrimmage and is therefore eligible for his Combat Infantry Badge, 1st Award. In addition, for being a part of a two round scrimmage in which both rounds were a victory he will receive the World War II Victory Medal.

    On the defense round of the EP3 Scrim, PFC A. Turner was the only member of the platoon assigned to the farm Southwest of the objective. The expectation was for him to be able to do some scouting and keep an eye on the SW approach should the enemy come that way. The intent was that if there was a Southwestern approach, he would fall back to the objective. Neither myself or PFC Turner imagined the speed and aggression that the enemy attacked with. He barely had enough time to call out what was happening before the enemy was on top of him with an MRAP at the farm. Having the opportunity to kill the MRAP gunner, he held his fire to not scare the enemy into falling back with their MRAP. This gave the tank the ability to kill the MRAP and its occupants. As another MRAP dismounted, PFC Turner threw a grenade at the enemy, killing three. The only friendly in the area other than PFC Turner was killed, but the PFC stayed alive, killing two enemies leaving farm to join in the push on the objective. With more enemies still in the area, and friendly reinforcements being killed beside him, PFC Turner continued calling out the few enemy remaining positions until they were eliminated. His composure while surrounded by chaos and ability to both survive and kill enemies was a key part of the effective defense.

  • Maj. Fritz
    May 2020

    On Saturday, April 25th, soldiers of Easy Company, Third Platoon, lead by 2Lt. Kelso deployed against the Canadian Forces. The two forces engaged on the hillsides and villages of Yehorivka, fighting to destroy a communications emplacement in the basement of a school building.

    Attack Round

    Deploying at the intersection roughly 2km east of the objective, EP3 climbed onto their vehicles and began driving north. Following main highways and village roads, EP3 after a few minutes of traveling, they disembarked their transports and behind cover a ridgeline, waiting for the order to attack. Our attached tank crew, lead by FSgt. Dethfield began opening fire with high explosive shells over a distance of 900 meters at the CF's ridgeline. An encampment and a silhouetted blue building received this fire with incredible accuracy. First blood was dropped here and the order for the advance began, but not before sufficient intelligence had been recovered of the Canadian Forces Deployment.

    To the far south, Sgt. Crane in a lone MATV drew the Canadian Forces attention long enough for EP3 to get into position. It was during this time that we found the location of the CF tank, giving us the clear that it was relatively safe for EP3 to begin to advance. With suppressive fire from our 50. Cal MG's and our Abrams, the squads of EP3 began their advance as directed by 2Lt. Kelso. Two to three hundred meters before the CF ridgeline, our forces came under heavy suppressive fire from machine gunners and riflemen of the Canadian Forces. EP3S3's leadership was wiped out in this action, forcing the remnants of that squad to fall into other squads as they fought to secure the ridgeline before the village that lead to the objective building. The advance as a whole was nearly jeopardized when the scout car arrived, shooting into our ranks before finally being neutralized.

    Progress was slow as the close quarters fighting heated up. On the west, 1Lt. Fritz and PFC Logan located the CF tank which was the prime concern for the success of the operation. PFC Logan took initiative in wounding the tank as our tank crew raced to get into the action. Continued communications between this small fire team and FSgt. Dethfield saw the Canadian Forces' T-72 obliterated.

    Pushing forward, EP3 got entangled in deadly building clearing. EP3S1 and EP3S2 suffered greatly as their ranks were reduced further. Thankfully, our practice in communications and our knowledge of the map paid off and we were able to overcome these obstacles. With the battle beginning to swing in our favor, the final push needed to be made. The school building was tight and condensed structure with small rooms and open windows. Suppressive fire was given before EP3 began clearing out the building room by room. When in place, Sgt. Crane who had survived his encounter with the tank from the beginning of contact and marched his way back to friendlies took over the assault, launching grenades and directing others in what to do. Eventually, C-4 was placed in the opening to the basement which killed one of the remaining CF soldiers. The last CF Soldier, Pte. Miller D., valiantly held his ground, wounding PFC Palladino but untimely falling dead to Palladino's grenade. With that, the clear was given and what remained of the greatly reduced platoon of EP3 hurriedly destroyed the radio.

    Defense Round

    2Lt. Kelso realized very early on in EP3's training that the best bet to success was to keep our defensive efforts internal. A few handpicked scouts were picked to secure and listen to the flanks for CF movement while the majority of EP3 held close to the town and the surrounding fields.

    The first few minutes passed without much action. All troops kept their ears and eyes peeled to their sections. There were a few call-outs here and there which were all proven false. It wasn't until roughly nine minutes in that the first contact was spotted to the south. Tank shells wizzed over a ravine back and forth as the Canadian Forces raced to get to the School building in a daring blitz. Shocked, but not deterred, orders from 2Lt. Kelso directed EP3S2 to reposition to meet the oncoming Canadain Forces.

    South of the main building, lies a small pond and a drainage pipe connected to a larger ditch. In here, Canadian Forces dismounted and began making their push. Thankfully for EP3, having secure interior lines, much of the platoon was able to redeploy and a heavy firefight began. A section of the CF forces veered off to the left flank and secured a farmhouse which was a threat to our defense. Supporting them was there Abrams tank which began engaged our Combat Engineers and T-72. With hasty coordination, the CF tank was destroyed, leaving a small section of CF troops isolated in that farmhouse. Initial contact with these last few soldiers ended poorly for the few 29th members engaged in the action. Within a few minutes, EP3 consolidated and then began systematically checking the area. After a few more exchanges of gunfire, the CF attack was brought to a halt, ending in victory for EP3.
